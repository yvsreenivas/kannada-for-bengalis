☕ How to Start a Tea & Snacks Stall

Startup cost: ₹10,000–₹30,000 · Daily earning potential: ₹500–₹1,500 profit · Best for: anyone willing to start early and work steady hours

Tea is the one business that never stops in Karnataka. Office workers, auto drivers, construction workers, students — everyone stops for a ₹10–₹15 cup. A small stall near a bus stop, market, hospital, or construction site can sell 100–300 cups a day. It is hard work, but the money is daily, in cash, and grows with your regular customers.

What you need to start

Basic setup (approximate):
  • Gas stove + cylinder + big kettle and vessels — ₹5,000–₹8,000
  • Pushcart or small fixed table setup — ₹5,000–₹15,000
  • Glasses/paper cups, tea powder, milk, sugar, ginger — ₹2,000 first stock
  • Snacks to resell (biscuits, buns, vada, bonda from a supplier) — ₹1,000–₹2,000
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city and year.

Step by step

  1. Pick the spot first. Stand at your planned location for two mornings and count the footfall. A good spot matters more than good tea.
  2. Get FSSAI basic registration. For small food businesses (turnover under ₹12 lakh/year) it costs about ₹100 per year. Apply online at foscos.fssai.gov.in — you need Aadhaar and a photo.
  3. Talk to the local authority. In cities like Bengaluru, street vendors should get a vending ID from the city corporation (BBMP). This protects you from being removed.
  4. Apply for PM SVANidhi. This central government scheme gives street vendors a collateral-free loan of ₹10,000 first, then ₹20,000, then ₹50,000 if you repay on time. Ask at any bank or Common Service Centre.
  5. Start simple. Tea, coffee, biscuits, buns. Add snacks only after you know what your customers ask for.

Where to buy your supplies

Your profit is decided at buying time, not selling time. Buy wholesale from day one:

  • Milk: tie up with the nearest Nandini (KMF) booth for a fixed daily quantity — booth operators give better rates for guaranteed daily purchase. A local dairy is the alternative.
  • Tea powder & sugar: wholesale provision shops — in Bengaluru, around KR Market and Chickpet. Buy one kilo of 2–3 tea brands first and let customers' reactions choose your blend.
  • Paper cups & covers: disposable-goods wholesale shops (Bengaluru: Chickpet / BVK Iyengar Road area), or B2B sites like IndiaMART for bulk rates.
  • Snacks: don't cook them yourself at first — tie up with a local bakery or condiment maker for daily supply, ideally with return-of-unsold terms.
  • Gas: take a commercial LPG connection from your local gas agency. Using a domestic cylinder for business is illegal and risks seizure and fines.
  • In any other city, the method is the same: ask a friendly existing stall owner "where do you buy?" — most will tell you proudly.

How the money works

One litre of milk plus tea powder and sugar makes roughly 12–15 small cups. Your cost per cup is around ₹5–₹7; you sell at ₹10–₹15. Selling 150 cups a day brings ₹1,500–₹2,000 revenue, and after milk, gas, and rent for the spot, ₹500–₹1,000 stays with you — every single day. UPI QR code (PhonePe/Google Pay) is a must: many customers carry no cash.

The Kannada you need at the stall

Customers come back to the tea seller who speaks to them warmly in Kannada. Learn these first:

ಬನ್ನಿ ಸಾರ್, ಟೀ ಬೇಕಾ?Banni sar, tea bēkā?
Come sir, do you want tea?
ಶುಗರ್ ಕಡಿಮೆ ಮಾಡ್ಲಾ?Sugar kaḍime māḍlā?
Shall I reduce the sugar?
ಒಂದು ಟೀ ಹತ್ತು ರೂಪಾಯಿOndu tea hattu rūpāyi
One tea is ten rupees
ಚಿಲ್ಲರೆ ಇದೆಯಾ?Chillare ideyā?

ಫೋನ್ ಪೇ ಮಾಡಿ ಸಾರ್PhonePe māḍi sar
Please pay by PhonePe, sir

Mistakes to avoid

  • Choosing a spot with no morning crowd because the rent is cheap.
  • Skipping FSSAI registration — the fine is far more than the ₹100 fee.
  • Giving unlimited credit ("khata") to strangers. Regulars only, small amounts.
  • Dirty glasses. Cleanliness is your cheapest advertisement.
