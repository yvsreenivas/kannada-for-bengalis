🚕 Ola, Uber, Rapido, Swiggy, Zepto & Blinkit Jobs

Startup cost: ₹0 (own vehicle) to ₹20,000/month (leased vehicle) · Monthly earning potential: ₹15,000–₹40,000 · Best for: anyone with a licence, a vehicle or the will to lease one, and a smartphone

No shop to rent, no stock to buy, no boss deciding your shift — apps like Ola, Uber, Rapido, Swiggy, Zomato, Zepto and Blinkit pay you to use your own vehicle on your own hours. The best-known route is becoming a cab captain for Ola, Uber, Rapido, or Bengaluru's zero-commission favourite Namma Yatri, but the same kind of apps also pay for bike-taxi rides, food delivery (Swiggy, Zomato), and grocery delivery (Zepto, Blinkit, Instamart). This guide covers cab captain in detail, then the other app-based options worth comparing.

What you need to start

If you already own a two-wheeler or car:
  • Valid driving licence for the vehicle class — ₹200–₹600 if you need to apply or renew
  • Aadhaar-linked bank account with UPI, and PAN card — free if you already have them
  • Smartphone with a data plan — ₹1,000–₹2,000/month if you don't already carry one
  • Commercial ("yellow board") registration — required only if you plan to run a car as a cab; most bike-taxi riders use their normal registration — ₹3,000–₈,000 in RTO fees and road tax difference
  • Commercial vehicle insurance (cars) — a few thousand rupees more per year than private insurance, but non-negotiable
If you don't own a vehicle:
  • Bike/scooter rental through a platform partner — roughly ₹150–₹300/day
  • Car lease-to-own schemes offered by Ola/Uber fleet partners — roughly ₹12,000–₹20,000/month, adjusted against a future purchase
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city, vehicle, and platform offers.

Step by step: becoming a cab captain

  1. Get the right licence first. A two-wheeler licence lets you do bike-taxi and delivery work. Driving a car as a cab needs a valid car licence; carrying paying passengers commercially technically needs a badge/permit endorsement from the RTO — check current requirements at your local RTO before you commit money to a car.
  2. Decide your vehicle category. Bike taxi has the lowest entry cost and highest earning-per-rupee-invested, but city rules on bike taxis change — confirm the current status in your city before buying a vehicle for this purpose. Auto and car cabs earn more per ride but cost more to enter and maintain.
  3. If it's a car, convert the registration. A private car cannot legally run as a paid cab. Converting to commercial ("yellow board") at the RTO, along with commercial insurance, is what protects you if there's an accident or a police check.
  4. Pick your platform(s). Ola and Uber take a commission per ride (historically around 15–25%, platforms change this often). Namma Yatri and some others use a flat daily/monthly subscription instead of commission — compare your actual take-home, not just the headline rate, and it's common for drivers to run two apps at once to reduce idle time.
  5. Complete onboarding. Upload licence, RC, insurance, and Aadhaar in the app; vehicle inspection and a background check follow. This usually takes a few days to a couple of weeks.
  6. Drive off-peak first. Your first week, learn the app, the surge patterns, and your city's traffic before committing to peak-hour, high-pressure driving.
  7. Track your real earnings weekly. Log fuel, maintenance, and commission separately from gross ride income — the gross number the app shows is not what you keep.

Other app-based jobs worth comparing

  • Delivery partner (Swiggy, Zomato, Zepto, Blinkit, Instamart): lower entry cost — a bike or bicycle is enough — and flexible hours, but per-order pay is small and depends heavily on order volume in your area.
  • Logistics/courier (Porter and similar): good for those with a larger vehicle (tempo, pickup van) — pays per trip, useful alongside another gig if you already own a load-carrying vehicle.
  • Skilled home services (Urban Company and similar): if you have a trade — electrician, plumber, salon, appliance repair — these platforms bring you customers directly without needing your own shop.
  • Warehouse/flexible delivery slots (Amazon Flex and similar): block-based delivery slots that fit around another job or gig.

How the money works

A bike-taxi or delivery rider doing 8–10 hours a day can gross ₹800–₹1,500 daily before fuel; after fuel and platform charges, ₹500–₹900 a day (₹15,000–₹27,000/month) is a realistic take-home for steady, full-time work. A car cab captain grosses more per ride but carries EMI/lease, fuel, and maintenance costs that eat a bigger slice — full-time car drivers commonly report ₹25,000–₹40,000/month after all costs, but this varies hugely by city, vehicle, and how many hours you actually drive. Peak hours and surge pricing matter more than total hours driven — four focused peak-hour shifts often beat ten scattered hours.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Running a car as a cab on private registration and private insurance — an accident or a routine check can leave you with no cover at all.
  • Judging your earnings by the app's gross number instead of what's left after fuel, commission, and maintenance.
  • Buying or leasing a vehicle before checking your city's current rules for that vehicle category — bike-taxi rules in particular have changed in some cities.
  • Driving 14–16 hours a day chasing every ride — burnout and accidents cost far more than the extra rides earn.
  • Relying on a single platform. Running two apps reduces idle time between rides without extra cost.

This article is general guidance, not legal or financial advice. Platform commissions, city vehicle rules, and RTO requirements change — confirm current details with your RTO and the platform before investing money.
