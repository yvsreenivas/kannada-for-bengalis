🧵 How to Start a Tailoring & Alterations Business

Startup cost: ₹8,000–₹25,000 · Monthly earning potential: ₹10,000–₹30,000 · Best for: patient, careful workers — men and women both

Ready-made clothes rarely fit perfectly. Pant length, kurta width, school uniforms, saree falls, zip replacement — alteration work walks in every single day, and it pays cash immediately. Many garment factory workers in Bengaluru already know stitching; turning that skill into your own small business is a natural next step.

Learn or sharpen the skill

If you already worked in a garment unit, you may only need practice on full garments. If you are new, tailoring courses run 3–6 months. Look for free or cheap training at PMKVY skill centres, government ITIs (Sewing Technology), and women's development programmes (Sthree Shakti groups often organise them). Private classes cost ₹5,000–₹15,000.

What you need to start

Basic setup (approximate):
  • Sewing machine (new manual: ₹8,000–₹12,000; motorised: ₹12,000–₹18,000; good second-hand: ₹4,000–₹7,000)
  • Scissors, measuring tape, chalk, threads, needles, buttons, zips — ₹1,500–₹3,000
  • Iron box (essential for finishing) — ₹1,000–₹2,000
  • A place to work: your room to start, later a small shop or a shared corner in a laundry/ironing shop
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city and machine model.

Step by step

  1. Start with alterations, not stitching new clothes. Alterations are quick, low-risk, and build trust: pant hemming ₹40–₹80, waist adjustment ₹60–₹120, zip change ₹50–₹100, kurta fitting ₹100–₹200.
  2. Work from your room first. Tell people in your building, your workplace, and nearby shops. Put a small board outside. WhatsApp status photos of finished work bring more customers than you expect.
  3. Partner with shops. Ready-made cloth shops and laundries constantly need an alteration tailor. Offer them a fixed rate; they send you daily work.
  4. Move to stitching new garments. Blouses, salwar sets, and uniforms pay ₹300–₹800 per piece. Festival seasons (Ugadi, Dasara, Deepavali, Eid, school reopening) bring waves of orders.
  5. Grow with a Mudra loan. A second machine (or an overlock/pico machine, ₹15,000–₹25,000) doubles what you can accept. The PM Mudra "Shishu" loan covers this with up to ₹50,000, collateral-free.

How the money works

Fifteen small alteration jobs a day at ₹50–₹80 average brings ₹750–₹1,200 daily. Thread and needles cost little; your real investment is time and accuracy. New-garment stitching pays more per piece but takes longer — most successful small tailors run both: alterations for daily cash, stitching orders for bigger amounts.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Promising same-day delivery and failing. A missed delivery date loses the customer forever.
  • Cutting before double-checking the measurement — cloth cannot be un-cut.
  • Underpricing to win customers. Cheap work attracts customers who never pay fairly.
  • Not writing orders down: keep a notebook with name, work, price, and delivery date.
