🥬 How to Start a Vegetable & Fruit Cart

Startup cost: ₹5,000–₹15,000 · Daily earning potential: ₹400–₹1,200 profit · Best for: early risers who like talking to people

Every family buys vegetables every day. A cart that comes to their street, with fresh stock and fair prices, becomes part of their routine. This is one of the cheapest businesses to start — the main investment is a cart and your first day's stock — and your money rotates daily: buy in the morning, sell by evening.

What you need to start

Basic setup (approximate):
  • Second-hand pushcart — ₹3,000–₹6,000 (new: ₹8,000–₹12,000)
  • Weighing scale (digital) — ₹1,000–₹1,500
  • First day's stock — ₹2,000–₹4,000
  • Plastic crates, covers, UPI QR code — ₹500
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city and season.

Step by step

  1. Learn the mandi first. Go to the nearest APMC yard (in Bengaluru: Yeshwanthpur, Kalasipalya, Binny Mill for fruits) at 5–6 am for a week. Watch what sells, note wholesale prices, and see who the honest traders are.
  2. Start with 8–10 fast-moving items. Tomato, onion, potato, greens, beans, chilli, coriander, lemon. Add fruits later once you can judge ripeness.
  3. Fix your route or spot. Either a fixed corner near homes, or a moving route through 3–4 residential streets at the same time every day. Same time, every day — that is how customers form the habit.
  4. Get a vending ID. Register as a street vendor with your city corporation. It protects your right to sell under the Street Vendors Act.
  5. Apply for PM SVANidhi. Street vendors get a collateral-free bank loan of ₹10,000, rising to ₹50,000 with good repayment — perfect for buying more stock in festival season.

Where to buy your supplies

  • Bengaluru: Yeshwanthpur APMC yard and Kalasipalya market for vegetables, Binny Mill / Anand Rao Circle side for fruits. HOPCOMS outlets sell at fixed rates when mandi prices spike.
  • Other Karnataka cities: every district town has an APMC yard — Bandipalya in Mysuru, Amargol in Hubballi, Baikampady in Mangaluru. Ask any auto driver where the wholesale mandi is; everyone knows.
  • Build a relationship with 2–3 traders, not one. Compare their rates daily for a week before opening a regular account (khata) with the most honest one — regulars get first pick of fresh stock and occasional credit.
  • Crates, covers, weighing scale: shops around the mandi itself sell used plastic crates and gunny sacks cheap; digital scales from general merchant wholesalers or IndiaMART.
  • Weekly santhe: village weekly markets near your area sometimes beat APMC prices for greens and seasonal items — worth one scouting trip.

How the money works

Typical margin on vegetables is 20–35% over the wholesale price. If you sell stock worth ₹3,000–₹4,000 a day, ₹600–₹1,200 stays with you after spoilage. Two rules protect your profit: buy only what you can sell in one day, and sell the last stock cheap in the evening instead of letting it rot. Fruits spoil faster but carry higher margins — learn them second, not first.

The Kannada you need at the cart

Housewives and grandmothers are your main customers, and they bargain in Kannada. If you can answer them in Kannada with a smile, you have won half the sale:

ತಾಜಾ ತರಕಾರಿ ಬಂದಿದೆ!Tājā tarakāri bandide!
Fresh vegetables have arrived!
ಟೊಮೇಟೊ ಕೆಜಿಗೆ ಮೂವತ್ತು ರೂಪಾಯಿTomato kejige mūvattu rūpāyi
Tomatoes are thirty rupees a kilo
ಎಷ್ಟು ಕೊಡ್ಲಿ ಅಮ್ಮ?Eṣṭu koḍli amma?
How much shall I give, madam?
ಅಷ್ಟು ಕಡಿಮೆ ಆಗಲ್ಲ ಅಮ್ಮAṣṭu kaḍime āgalla amma
It can't go that low, madam

Mistakes to avoid

  • Buying too much stock in week one. Spoilage eats beginners' profits.
  • Changing your route or timing often — customers can't find you.
  • One dishonest weighing. Word spreads in a street faster than you can push the cart.
  • Ignoring the evening crowd: office-returners buy quick vegetables at 6–8 pm.
