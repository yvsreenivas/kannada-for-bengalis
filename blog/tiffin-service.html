🍱 How to Start a Tiffin (Home Food) Service

Startup cost: ₹5,000–₹15,000 · Monthly earning potential: ₹12,000–₹35,000 · Best for: good cooks — a family effort works best

Here is your unfair advantage as a migrant: thousands of workers from your own state live around you, and they all miss home food. A Bengali worker in Bengaluru dreams of macher jhol and proper rice; an Odia worker misses dalma; a Gujarati misses rotli-shaak. Local hotels cannot cook it — you can. A tiffin service that delivers the taste of home builds fiercely loyal customers.

What you need to start

Basic setup (approximate):
  • Big cooking vessels, stove, cylinder (you may already have these) — ₹3,000–₹8,000
  • Tiffin carriers / disposable food boxes — ₹1,000–₹3,000 to start
  • First week's provisions (rice, dal, oil, spices, vegetables) — ₹2,000–₹4,000
  • FSSAI basic registration — about ₹100 per year
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city and menu.

Step by step

  1. Start with 5–10 customers you know. Roommates' friends, workmates, people from your state in nearby PGs and quarters. Do not print pamphlets on day one — cook well for ten people first.
  2. Get FSSAI basic registration. Any food business needs it. For turnover under ₹12 lakh/year, it is a simple online registration at foscos.fssai.gov.in for about ₹100/year — Aadhaar and a photo are enough.
  3. Fix a simple weekly menu. One veg and one non-veg option maximum. A fixed menu keeps your buying cheap and your cooking fast. Monthly plans (for example ₹2,200–₹3,200 for lunch, ₹4,000–₹6,000 for both meals) give you advance cash to buy provisions.
  4. Solve delivery early. Within walking distance, deliver yourself; beyond that, cluster customers in one area or make them collect from one point (a friendly shop). Delivery time discipline decides whether customers stay.
  5. Grow through WhatsApp. A daily status photo of the day's meal is your entire marketing budget. Happy customers forward it to their friends from the same state.

Where to buy your supplies

  • Rice, dal, oil, atta: buy monthly from grain wholesalers — in Bengaluru, the APMC grain yard and KR Market side shops. Monthly wholesale buying saves 15–25% over the local kirana store.
  • Vegetables: the early-morning wholesale mandi (Kalasipalya or Yeshwanthpur in Bengaluru) — the same trip other food businesses make. Buying for 30 meals at retail prices will eat your whole margin.
  • Home-state spices and specialties: shops run by your own community stock them (mustard oil, panch phoron, specific rice varieties) — ask in your community's WhatsApp groups where they buy; buying a group order together cuts the price further.
  • Disposable boxes and carriers: disposable-goods wholesalers (Chickpet area in Bengaluru) — start with hundreds, not thousands, until your packaging size is settled.
  • Gas: a business cooking daily should take a commercial LPG connection — a domestic cylinder used for business risks seizure and fines.

How the money works

A home-style meal costs ₹35–₹55 in ingredients and sells for ₹70–₹120. Thirty lunch customers on monthly plans bring roughly ₹70,000–₹90,000 revenue; after provisions, gas, and boxes, a well-run kitchen keeps ₹20,000–₹35,000 a month. The biggest cost killer is waste — cook against confirmed orders, not hope. Take a small advance or weekly payment; month-end collection from thirty people is a job in itself.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Taking more customers than your stove can cook. Quality drops, and word spreads.
  • Changing taste or quantity after the first month — that is when customers quit.
  • Skipping FSSAI registration. It is ₹100; a complaint without it is expensive.
  • Free credit for months. Weekly or advance payment, politely but firmly.
