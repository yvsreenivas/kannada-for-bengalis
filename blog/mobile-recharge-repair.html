📱 How to Start a Mobile Recharge & Repair Shop

Startup cost: ₹30,000–₹1,00,000 · Monthly earning potential: ₹15,000–₹40,000 · Best for: those who enjoy technology and careful hand work

Everyone has a phone, and every phone eventually breaks — cracked screens, dead batteries, charging problems. Mobile repair is a skill you learn once and carry for life, in any city in India. You can start small with recharges and accessories, learn repair on the side, and grow into a full shop.

Learn the skill first

Repair is the part that pays. A basic mobile repair course takes 2–3 months and costs ₹10,000–₹25,000 at private institutes in cities like Bengaluru, Hubballi, or Mysuru. Government options are cheaper: look for mobile repair courses under PMKVY (Pradhan Mantri Kaushal Vikas Yojana) skill centres — often free — or nearby ITI short courses. Until then, you can already earn from recharges, SIM sales, and accessories, which need no training.

What you need to start

Basic setup (approximate):
  • Small rented shop or shared counter — deposit ₹10,000–₹50,000 depending on area
  • Glass counter, chair, signboard — ₹8,000–₹15,000
  • Accessories stock (covers, chargers, earphones, tempered glass) — ₹10,000–₹20,000
  • Repair tool kit + soldering station + spare parts — ₹8,000–₹15,000
  • Recharge float money (kept in your retailer app wallet) — ₹2,000–₹5,000
All figures are indicative — prices vary by city and area.

Step by step

  1. Start earning with recharges and accessories. Register as a retailer with distributor apps for Airtel/Jio/Vi recharges. Margin is small (2–4%) but it brings people into your shop every day.
  2. Add SIM card sales. Telecom distributors pay commission per activation. You will need your own KYC documents in order.
  3. Learn and add repairs. Screen and battery replacement are 70% of all repair work. A screen change can earn ₹200–₹500 labour on top of the part cost.
  4. Take a trade license. A shop needs a trade license from the city corporation and, if your turnover grows, GST registration. Keep purchase bills for all spare parts — it protects you from stolen-parts trouble.
  5. Grow with a Mudra loan. The PM Mudra Yojana "Shishu" loan gives up to ₹50,000 collateral-free for exactly this kind of shop; "Kishor" goes higher. Apply through any bank with your business plan.

Where to buy your supplies

  • Accessories wholesale: in Bengaluru, SP Road (Sadar Patrappa Road) and National Market are where every small mobile shop in Karnataka buys covers, chargers, tempered glass, and earphones. Prices drop sharply when you buy 10–20 pieces of an item.
  • Spare parts: also SP Road for screens, batteries, and charging boards; online, parts sites like Maxbhi ship model-specific parts to any town. Always match the exact model number before ordering.
  • Recharge & SIM distributorship: ask two or three existing shops which Airtel/Jio/Vi distributor covers your area, and call him directly. Retailer platforms like PayNearby also bundle recharges, bill payments, and money transfer in one app — each service earns a small commission.
  • B2B online: Udaan and IndiaMART list accessory wholesalers with delivery — useful when you cannot travel to the city market, but check per-piece rates against SP Road before ordering big.
  • Rule for stock: visit the wholesale market once, note the 20 fastest-moving items for the phone models common in your area, and buy only those. Dead stock is where new shops lose money.

How the money works

Accessories carry the best margins — a phone cover bought at ₹40 wholesale sells for ₹100–₹150. Repairs earn labour charges daily. Recharges earn little but guarantee footfall. A settled shop in a worker-area or near a college typically clears ₹15,000–₹40,000 a month after rent. Trust is everything: quote the price before opening the phone, and return the old parts to the customer.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Buying big accessory stock before knowing which phone models your area uses.
  • Taking repair jobs beyond your skill and returning a dead phone — one angry customer costs ten new ones.
  • Dealing in phones without bills. Never buy or sell a phone without proof of ownership.
  • Keeping no written job card — always note the phone's condition when the customer hands it over.
